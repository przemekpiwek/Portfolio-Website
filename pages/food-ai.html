Food AI

Food AI is a project I completed with one other teammate as part of a tech-innovation competition, Cooperathon. It aims to reduce environmental footprint by managing culinary inventory and providing tailored recipes for expiring food. Unlike other cooking apps, the product combines personalized recipe generation and pantry management so that you can save money, eat healthier, and live sustainably.

THE CHALLENGE

If "food waste" were a country, it would be the third-largest emitter of greenhouse gases. In Canada alone, this represents a $29 billion of value lost along the supply chain and 193 million metric tonnes of carbon dioxide equivalent emitted. What is equally surprising, household behavior is responsible for 40% of the food wasted with two-thirds being avoidable. My high-level goal was to create a cooking application that provides a solution to this problem.

DESIGN PROCESS

In the design process, I followed a user-centered approach and applied Design-Thinking methodology.

Empathize

I began my process browsing existing applications and reading their reviews in the App Store and Google Play stores. This gave me insight into pain points, features people liked, and where to strategically position FoodAI. I discovered the market is dividing into two clusters: one that focuses on pantry management and expiry reminders (i.e. Beep Noise, Fridgly), and the other on recipe generation (i.e. Glouton, Mealime). Currently, no player offers a solution where these two markets intersect - and this presents an ideal opportunity for FoodAI.

What the app reviews told me:​

  • Cooking is a personalized experience - people need options based on their specific needs (i.e. dietary preferences, filters, favorites, etc.)
  • People don't want lots of recipes. High ratings are a major decision driver.
  • People hate disruptions to the cooking process (i.e. pop-up videos, ads, long introductions, instructions, etc.)

To find out more about my potential users, I launched an online survey with a focus on finding:​

  • What is my key demographic?
  • What are the tasks associated with cooking?
  • Why do consumers waste food?
  • What pain points are associated with the cooking experience?
  • What features are people willing to pay for?
  • Why do consumers choose to cook?
  • What are the drivers for food selection?

Data gathered from 80 respondents suggested my prioritized customer segment is flexitarian (semi-vegetarian) Millenials; this group is actively seeking ways to reduce their carbon footprint and are willing to pay for features that accomplished that. With this information, I had an idea of my end-user and conducted several interviews and contextual inquiries with candidates who fit this profile.

KEY FEATURES

Grocery Receipt Scanner - Users can scan their grocery receipts. Using an Optical Character Recognition API, the food items are identified and categorized in their inventory.

Inventory Manager with Expiration - Users can keep track of their fridge and pantry contents in their inventory, where an expiration model predicts which foods are expiring first and notifies the user.

Personalized Quiz before Meals - Users can choose to interact with a short quiz before deciding what to cook to gauge their immediate culinary preferences, mood, and needs. This reduces friction points for the user and opens the possibility of future revenue-generating streams, such as selling market intelligence to grocery stores.

Smart Recipe Generator - The user is presented with healthy and delicious meals based on the recommendations of a machine learning algorithm, which prioritizes their culinary preferences, inventory content, and expiring foods.

Meal Metrics - Users are informed about their meals through a weekly report that gauges the carbon emissions associated with their meals and a sustainability score that will incentivize users to be more conscious about their eating habits. They have the option of offsetting their emissions, which can be achieved by planting trees (the average cost of carbon offsetting for a North American is approximately $3 per user, annually).

To ensure all user actions are accounted for, I created a User Flow and Site Map for the application.

THINGS I LEARNED:

After speaking with programmers with ML experience, I discovered the accuracy of the O.C.R API would hover around 80%, with a maximum value of around 90%. Subsequent usability studies revealed manual entry of food items would be considered a major pain point and could present feasibility issues. Moreover, building a model for each specific user would require lots of training data, which means the user realizes maximum value only after long-term continued use. In the future, collaboration with technical leads early on would have prevented these issues.
